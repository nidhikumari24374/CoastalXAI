\section{\textbf{INTRODUCTION}}

\subsection{\textbf{Background and Motivation}}

Climate change has evolved from being a distant scientific concern to a
daily reality affecting millions of people. Rising temperatures, melting
ice caps, and shifting rainfall patterns are widely discussed, but one of
the most fragile and high-risk zones on the planet is the \textit{coastal
interface} between land and ocean. Coastal regions host dense human
settlements, ports, industries and critical infrastructure. Even small
changes in temperature or sea level can trigger disproportionate social,
economic and ecological consequences.

Asia, in particular, is home to several mega-deltas (such as the Ganges–
Brahmaputra, Mekong and Yangtze), low-lying island nations and
rapidly expanding coastal cities. These regions face simultaneous threats
from \textbf{land submergence}, \textbf{sea-level rise} and \textbf{extreme heat}. The
interaction of these variables is non-linear: a minor change in land
impact or coastal length can, under certain conditions, lead to a major
change in coastal temperature. This makes purely manual analysis
insufficient and motivates the use of data-driven modelling.

At the same time, policy makers, coastal engineers and environmental
planners are cautious about adopting complex machine-learning models
because they often behave like ``black boxes''. A model that predicts a
future temperature but cannot justify \emph{why} it produced that number
cannot be fully trusted in high-stakes decisions such as the design of
sea walls, zoning regulations or relocation of vulnerable communities.
This tension between accuracy and interpretability sets the stage for the
\textbf{CoastalXAI} project.

\subsection{\textbf{Coastal Climate Challenges in Asia}}

The Asian coastline spans arid regions, tropical monsoon zones and
temperate climates. Some countries, such as Bahrain and the United Arab
Emirates, exhibit high temperatures with small land areas and limited
fresh-water resources. Others, such as India, Bangladesh or Vietnam,
have long and heavily populated coastlines that are exposed to cyclones,
storm surges and riverine flooding. In many locations, \textbf{land
subsidence} due to groundwater extraction or sediment compaction causes
the land surface to sink relative to sea level, effectively accelerating
submergence.

Key challenges faced by Asian coastal regions include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Rising mean temperatures}, which increase the likelihood of
  marine heatwaves and urban heat-island effects.
  \item \textbf{Changing coastline geometry}, captured through metrics such as
  coastal length and land area, which modifies how heat and moisture
  are exchanged between land and sea.
  \item \textbf{Anthropogenic land impact}, including land-use change,
  reclamation projects and erosion, which further alter local micro-climates.
  \item \textbf{Limited, noisy environmental data} for some countries,
  making it difficult to build robust models without careful
  preprocessing and feature engineering.
\end{itemize}

Traditional statistical techniques struggle to capture these coupled
effects, especially when the relationships among variables are
non-linear and involve complex interactions. This motivates the adoption
of ensemble machine-learning methods, which can learn intricate
patterns from data.

\subsection{\textbf{Role of Machine Learning in Environmental Modelling}}

Machine-learning (ML) techniques such as Support Vector Machines,
Random Forests and Neural Networks have demonstrated strong
performance in various environmental applications, including rainfall
prediction, flood forecasting and coastal erosion modelling. ML models
are particularly well suited to:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Handling \textbf{multi-dimensional feature spaces} with both
  numerical and categorical variables.
  \item Capturing \textbf{non-linear relationships} between drivers
  (e.g. land impact, coastal length, sea level) and responses
  (temperature, erosion rate, etc.).
  \item Exploiting \textbf{historical data} to learn patterns without
  requiring an explicit analytical equation for every process.
\end{itemize}

Among these models, the \textbf{Random Forest Regressor} has become a
popular choice for tabular environmental data. It aggregates multiple
decision trees, thereby reducing variance and improving generalisation
\cite{breiman2001}. Random Forests are relatively robust to outliers,
scaling differences and missing values, which makes them attractive for
real-world datasets that are imperfect and noisy.

However, the major criticism of many ML approaches is their lack of
transparency. A model may fit the data extremely well, but if it
cannot explain why a particular combination of land area, country and
sea level leads to a given temperature prediction, domain experts may
remain sceptical. This challenge directly motivates the integration of
Explainable AI (XAI) techniques into CoastalXAI.

\subsection{\textbf{Need for Explainable AI in Climate Decision Making}}

Explainable AI (XAI) refers to a family of methods that make ML models
more interpretable and transparent to human users. In climate and
environmental applications, interpretability is not just a nice
add-on—it is a \textbf{core requirement}. When predictions are used as
inputs to policy or long-term infrastructure planning, stakeholders need
answers to questions such as:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Which factors are most responsible for the predicted warming in a
  given coastal region?
  \item Are the model's conclusions consistent with physical intuition and
  established scientific literature?
  \item How sensitive is the prediction to changes in land-use or sea-level
  scenarios?
\end{itemize}

Two widely adopted XAI methods are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations)} \cite{shap2017}, which
  uses concepts from cooperative game theory to attribute a
  prediction to individual features. SHAP values offer a consistent
  notion of feature contribution and can be visualised through
  force plots and waterfall plots.
  \item \textbf{LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations)}
  \cite{lime2016}, which builds a simple surrogate model (often a
  linear regressor) in the neighbourhood of a single prediction. This
  helps users understand \textit{locally} why the model behaved in a certain
  way for one particular input.
\end{itemize}

By combining Random Forest regression with SHAP and LIME, the
CoastalXAI system aims to deliver both \textbf{high predictive accuracy}
and \textbf{human-friendly explanations}. In particular, the system is designed
so that a user can not only view a numeric temperature prediction but
also see which features pushed the value upward or downward and by
how much.

\subsection{\textbf{Problem Statement}}

Despite advances in environmental ML research, several gaps remain
between academic models and practical tools deployed for coastal
planning:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\alph*)]
  \item \textbf{Accuracy–Interpretability Gap:} Many existing studies use
  either simple, interpretable models (e.g.\ linear regression) with
  limited accuracy, or highly complex models (e.g.\ deep neural
  networks) that act as black boxes. There is a need for a system
  that balances both accuracy and interpretability using modern XAI
  techniques.
  \item \textbf{Fragmented Workflows:} Researchers often train models in
  isolated notebooks, manually generate plots and do not package the
  workflow into a reusable application. Stakeholders without coding
  expertise cannot easily experiment with new scenarios.
  \item \textbf{Limited Scenario Exploration:} Existing tools typically
  provide static predictions for historical years. Policy makers,
  however, require \textbf{what-if analysis} (e.g.\ ``What if land impact
  increases while sea level continues to rise?''), future trend
  simulation and comparison between multiple countries.
  \item \textbf{Lack of Narrative Explanations:} Most XAI outputs are
  presented as technical plots. Non-technical users, such as local
  administrative officers, may prefer short, textual summaries in
  plain language that tell a ``climate story'' for a given region.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
\textbf{Formal Problem Statement:}\\[0.1cm]
\textit{Design and develop an Explainable AI based decision-support system
for predicting coastal temperature under varying land submergence and
sea-level conditions for Asian countries, such that the system (i)
achieves high predictive accuracy, (ii) provides transparent global and
local explanations for each prediction, and (iii) allows users to
interactively explore scenarios, trends and country-wise comparisons.}

\subsection{\textbf{Objectives}}

To address the above problem, the CoastalXAI project is guided by the
following objectives:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Data Engineering:} Curate, clean and preprocess a
  multi-country coastal dataset spanning 1990–2020, including
  features such as land area, coastal length, land impact, sea level,
  annual balance and country identifiers.
  \item \textbf{Model Development:} Implement and fine-tune a Random
  Forest Regression model that achieves at least \mbox{$R^2 > 0.95$} on the
  test set for temperature prediction, inspired by the methodology of
  \cite{wang2024}.
  \item \textbf{Explainability Integration:} Integrate SHAP and LIME
  to provide both global feature importance (across the full dataset)
  and local explanations (for individual predictions), visualised
  through force plots, waterfall plots and bar charts.
  \item \textbf{Interactive Web Interface:} Develop a Streamlit-based
  frontend that allows users to:
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\roman*)]
    \item Input custom scenarios via sliders and dropdowns.
    \item View predictions in real time with dynamic colour-coded
    feedback.
    \item Explore XAI visualisations for each scenario.
  \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Advanced Analytical Features:} Extend the basic dashboard
  with innovative capabilities, including:
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Future Climate Simulation Mode}: varying the year
    from historical (1990) to near-future horizons (up to 2050)
    while keeping other factors fixed, to understand predicted
    trends.
    \item \textbf{Automatic Climate Report Generator}: automatic
    generation of a PDF summary containing prediction values,
    key drivers and risk assessments.
    \item \textbf{Country Comparison Mode}: side-by-side comparison of
    two countries under the same environmental configuration to
    identify differential feature contributions.
    \item \textbf{Climate Story Mode}: rule-based generation of a
    human-readable narrative explanation for each prediction,
    targeting non-technical users.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{\textbf{Scope of the Project}}

The scope of CoastalXAI is carefully defined to keep the project
manageable while still providing meaningful insights:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Geographical Scope:} The dataset and analysis are
  restricted to countries within the Asian continent. This focus
  aligns with the reference research paper \cite{wang2024} and
  reflects Asia's high vulnerability to coastal hazards.
  \item \textbf{Temporal Scope:} The core model is trained on historical
  data from 1990 to 2020. The ``future simulation'' module extends
  predictions to 2050, but these values are presented as
  \textit{trend-based extrapolations}, not certified climate
  projections.
  \item \textbf{Variable Scope:} The primary output variable is coastal
  temperature (in $^\circ$C). Other climate variables such as
  precipitation, wind speed or humidity are outside the present
  scope but can be integrated in future work.
  \item \textbf{Technological Scope:} The project is a pure software
  solution built using Python, scikit-learn, SHAP, LIME and
  Streamlit. It does not include physical sensors, IoT devices or
  real-time data acquisition hardware.
  \item \textbf{User Scope:} The designed interface targets environmental
  researchers, engineering students, urban planners and policy
  analysts who may not have deep expertise in programming but are
  comfortable interpreting graphs and structured reports.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{\textbf{Significance of the Study}}

The significance of CoastalXAI can be viewed from multiple
perspectives:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Academic Significance:} The project demonstrates how an
  advanced ensemble model can be tightly integrated with SHAP and
  LIME to create a transparent prediction pipeline. It converts a
  research-paper methodology into an end-to-end software artefact
  that others can extend.
  \item \textbf{Practical Significance:} Coastal planners can use the
  system to quickly test ``what-if'' scenarios, such as modifying
  land impact or exploring alternative coastline configurations,
  and immediately see how the predicted temperature and its
  explanations change.
  \item \textbf{Pedagogical Significance:} The project doubles as an
  educational tool for students learning machine learning and XAI.
  By observing how changes in input parameters alter both the
  predictions and the SHAP/LIME explanations, learners gain an
  intuitive understanding of feature importance.
  \item \textbf{Policy Support:} Although not a replacement for full
  climate models, CoastalXAI can inform preliminary decision-making
  by highlighting which factors most strongly influence thermal
  risk in different countries and how these relationships vary
  across scenarios.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{\textbf{Methodology Overview}}

At a high level, the CoastalXAI workflow consists of two tightly
connected pipelines:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Offline Training Pipeline}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Load and clean the raw \texttt{Land Submergence dataset.csv},
      filtering for Asian countries and years between 1990 and 2020.
      \item Handle missing values and outliers; perform one-hot encoding
      for categorical features such as \texttt{Country} and \texttt{Continent}.
      \item Split the data into training and test sets; train the Random
      Forest Regressor and evaluate its performance.
      \item Save the trained model and associated artefacts (feature
      column list, LIME training data, categorical indices and
      pre-generated SHAP plots) to disk as \texttt{.joblib}, \texttt{.json}
      and \texttt{.csv} files.
    \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Online Inference and Interpretation Pipeline}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Load the saved model and artefacts in a Streamlit frontend.
      \item Accept user input through sliders and dropdown menus for
      year, country, land area, coastal length, land impact, sea
      level and annual balance.
      \item Construct a feature vector consistent with the training
      schema and compute the predicted temperature.
      \item Generate SHAP and LIME explanations for the given input,
      and display them through force plots, waterfall plots and
      local bar charts.
      \item Provide additional analytical modes such as future trend
      simulation, PDF report generation, country comparison and
      natural-language climate stories.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

For visual clarity, the detailed architecture and data-flow diagrams
are presented in Chapter~4 (System Design).

\section{\textbf{LITERATURE SURVEY}}

The adoption of machine learning and artificial intelligence in environmental modelling has evolved significantly over the last decade. Traditional environmental studies relied heavily on statistical regression, trend analysis techniques and physics-driven equations. While such models are interpretable, they fail to capture complex interactions among variables such as sea level, land area, coastline geometry and anthropogenic land modifications. This literature survey reviews prior works in three major domains relevant to this project: (a) climate and coastal-temperature prediction models, (b) implementation of advanced regression algorithms for coastal datasets and (c) transparency methods for explaining model outputs through Explainable AI (XAI).

\subsection{\textbf{2.1 Coastal and Temperature Modelling Studies}}

Studies focusing on coastal vulnerability predominantly analyse erosion, thermal changes, sea-level rise and land subsidence. According to Wang et al. (2024) \cite{wang2024}, coastal temperature variation correlates significantly with variables such as land area, annual mass balance changes and coastline displacement. Their study explored 30 years of temperature and land-change data and concluded that coastal environments — especially in dense coastal belts — show rapid thermal shifts that cannot be captured by simplistic forecasting models.

Similarly, Ghosh et al. (2019) \cite{ghosh2019} applied Support Vector Machines to coastal erosion forecasting and demonstrated that machine learning performs substantially better than deterministic regression-based formulas. However, their work lacked the ability to display how different environmental variables influenced predictions — which has implications for decision-making.

Across multiple studies, a consistent pattern emerges:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Climate variables exhibit strong seasonal and long-term non-linear behaviour.
    \item The interaction between land-use change and sea-level contributes significantly to coastal micro-temperature zones.
    \item Predictive systems require multi-parameter input with robust processing rather than univariate extrapolation.
\end{itemize}

This motivates machine-learning-based modelling with multi-feature explainability — which CoastalXAI implements.

\subsection{\textbf{2.2 Machine Learning in Environmental Prediction}}

The work of Breiman (2001) \cite{breiman2001} formally introduced Random Forest as a general-purpose regression and classification technique capable of dealing with noisy multidimensional datasets. Multiple later studies acknowledge its suitability for environmental prediction because:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item It reduces overfitting through random feature sampling at tree-splits.
    \item It stabilises prediction variance through ensemble aggregation.
    \item It remains robust when physical meaning of features is heterogeneous.
\end{enumerate}

In the climate space, Venkatesan et al. (2017) \cite{venk2017} implemented Random Forest for marine environment variability, concluding that non-linear feature interactions are dominant drivers of ecological changes. This aligns closely with the complexity of the dataset used in CoastalXAI.

Several works on temperature prediction, including NOAA reports (2021-2024) and urban-heat-island detection systems, emphasise that the best-performing models are ensemble-based rather than interpretable linear regression systems. However, unlike physical modelling frameworks, machine-learning models are criticised because they lack transparency. Such models predict accurately, but decision-makers cannot validate whether such predictions arise from appropriate cause–effect relationships.

\subsection{\textbf{2.3 Land Submergence, Sea-Level Rise and Thermal Impact}}

Across global coastal studies, three environmental variables frequently appear as strong drivers of climatic change:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Land subsidence / land-impact factor}
    \item \textbf{Net coastline expansion or reduction}
    \item \textbf{Gradual sea-level rise}
\end{enumerate}

The research paper used in this project \cite{wang2024} was among the first Modern Asian studies that attempted to quantify coastal temperature shifts based on:

\begin{itemize}
    \item land impact quantification
    \item coastline geometric variation
    \item continental aggregation
    \item annual-mass balance alterations
\end{itemize}

Their findings revealed that countries like Bahrain, Qatar and Oman suffered accelerated thermal shifts despite smaller relative land sizes — explained by:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Low inland vegetation
    \item Higher reflective radiation
    \item Higher energy retained due to water land boundaries
\end{itemize}

These findings directly influenced the design of CoastalXAI parameters.

\subsection{\textbf{2.4 Explainability Techniques in ML (SHAP and LIME)}} 

Instead of treating ML predictions as final outputs, modern XAI (Explainable AI) frameworks reveal reasoning behind predictions.

\subsubsection{\textbf{Local and Global Explainability Gap}}

Before SHAP and LIME existed, model explanations were either:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Global feature importance scores (e.g., impurity-based feature ranking)
    \item Local sensitivity or what-if change metrics
\end{itemize}

SHAP \cite{shap2017} addresses global interpretability by ensuring:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Mathematical fairness of contribution distribution  
    \item Additivity consistency  
    \item Feature-wise marginal contribution estimation  
\end{itemize}

This technique visualises how much each feature increases or decreases the prediction.

LIME \cite{lime2016} provides localised sanity-checked interpretations by:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Perturbing (slightly altering) a small neighbourhood of inputs
    \item Fitting a simple linear regression surrogate
    \item Explaining the prediction with weighted features
\end{itemize}

Together they solve:

\begin{center}
    \textbf{“What influenced the model globally?”} → SHAP  

    \textbf{“Why did THIS prediction occur?”} → LIME
\end{center}

Both frameworks are implemented in CoastalXAI after model prediction.

\subsection{\textbf{2.5 Identified Research Gap}}

From collective literature, three major gaps emerge:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Absence of interactive analytical tools}—existing studies produce static graphs, not operational applications.
    \item \textbf{Little focus on transparency for individual predictions}, which is critical for environmental regulation.
    \item \textbf{Insufficient scenario-based predictions} where user can vary features dynamically.
\end{enumerate}

Therefore, the present work contributes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Real-time prediction engine (Streamlit)
    \item LIME-enabled local interpretability
    \item SHAP-based heat maps + waterfall visualisation
    \item Future temperature simulation (1990-2050 horizon)
    \item Auto-report generation mode
    \item Country-comparison analytics
    \item Human-readable narrative summarisation
\end{itemize}

Such capability is not reported in existing research tools — turning CoastalXAI into a novel academic and technical contribution.

\section{\textbf{CONCLUSION AND FUTURE ENHANCEMENTS}}

\subsection{\textbf{Conclusion}}

Climate change modelling has traditionally been dominated by linear and statistical approaches, which often fail to capture complex non-linear interactions among environmental variables. The proposed project, \textbf{CoastalXAI: Temperature Prediction and Interpretation System}, successfully bridges this gap by integrating a highly accurate Random Forest Regression model with state-of-the-art Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) frameworks.

The achieved performance metrics indicate that the model was able to capture more than 96\% variance in target temperature values, which is significantly higher than traditional regression-based climate models. More importantly, prediction accuracy was not the sole objective; the emphasis on interpretability differentiates this system from existing climate forecasting solutions.

Key strengths of the developed system include:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{High predictive reliability} achieved using an ensemble-based architecture.
    \item \textbf{Human-readable explanation} of each prediction using SHAP and LIME.
    \item \textbf{Real-time inference capability} through a highly interactive Streamlit interface.
    \item \textbf{Scenario simulation feature} allowing users to analyse temporal temperature trends extending up to the year 2050.
    \item \textbf{Country-to-country comparative insight} indicating geographic influences in climate behaviour.
    \item \textbf{Automated climate summary report generation}, replicating government-grade assessment documentation.
\end{enumerate}

The project demonstrates that even for domains such as climatology—where prediction is probabilistic and uncertainty-driven—modern machine learning with XAI reconciles both transparency and correctness. This is one of the primary contributions of CoastalXAI.

The integration of explanations assures that the model does not function as a black-box predictive engine. Instead, stakeholders can trust outputs because every contributing factor is clearly revealed in terms of positive or negative effect. For instance, land impact effects, sea-level variation influence, or country-specific attributes appear visually and numerically quantified within the dashboard.

Additionally, the project promotes climate awareness by converting model output into narrative summaries. This functionality is rare even among published research systems and demonstrates innovation because policymakers benefit more from textual advisory-style reporting than statistical graphs.


% ==========================================
\subsection{\textbf{Research Significance}}
% ==========================================

The significance of CoastalXAI extends beyond being a mini-project demonstration. It showcases the ability of AI-based decision models to:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Assist climate analysts in identifying geographic thermodynamic pressure points.
    \item Anticipate possible environmental risk factors associated with coastal regions.
    \item Support urban-planning departments during zoning-level expansion work.
    \item Inform marine ecosystem-impact assessments using objective prediction logic.
\end{itemize}

Through SHAP and LIME, complex environmental relations—such as how land area and coastal length jointly influence heat accumulation—are made explicit. This adds academic, industrial and ecological value to the proposed model.


% ==========================================
\subsection{\textbf{Limitations of Present Work}}
% ==========================================

Despite its strengths, certain limitations remain:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The dataset used represents Asian countries only; global applicability requires region-wise retraining.
    \item The model assumes static input parameters; however, environmental conditions evolve dynamically over time.
    \item The dataset volume remains moderate; greater density of temporal records can further increase reliability.
    \item Inference results beyond the training years (post-2020) represent predictive extrapolation and may require adaptive recalibration.
\end{itemize}

These limitations create meaningful direction for future expansion.
